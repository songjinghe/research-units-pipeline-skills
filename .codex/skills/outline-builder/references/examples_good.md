# Good Examples

## Good subsection bullet set

- `Intent:` explain what belongs in the subsection and how it differs from adjacent subtopics.
- `RQ:` identify the decision-relevant trade-off the subsection answers.
- `Evidence needs:` list mechanism, evaluation protocol, and failure modes.
- `Expected cites:` state the expected citation density or citation types.
- `Concrete comparisons:` name the A vs B contrasts that must appear later.
- `Evaluation anchors:` name benchmarks, datasets, metrics, or protocols.
- `Comparison axes:` keep the axes as short atomic phrases separated by semicolons.

## Good chapter framing

- chapter bullets summarize the organizing logic and chapter-level evidence needs
- front sections (`Introduction`, `Related Work`) remain generic enough to fit multiple domains
- taxonomy-driven chapters inherit titles and scope from the input taxonomy rather than from hard-coded survey domains
