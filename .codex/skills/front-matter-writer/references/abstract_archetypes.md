# Abstract Archetypes

Use these as decision patterns, not fill-in-the-blank templates.

## Choose one primary archetype

### 1) Tension-first

Use when:
- the field is broad but comparisons are unstable
- the survey’s main value is clarifying a recurring trade-off

Jobs:
- state the comparison problem
- name the lens that stabilizes comparison
- close with what the reader learns from that lens

### 2) Lens-first

Use when:
- the contribution is mainly organizational or taxonomic
- the survey unifies several neighboring strands under one frame

Jobs:
- define the lens early
- show which strands become comparable under it
- end with the key consequence for evaluation or interpretation

### 3) Protocol-first

Use when:
- results in the literature depend strongly on budgets, tools, environments, or threat models
- the survey’s novelty is evaluation-aware positioning

Jobs:
- foreground comparability conditions
- connect architecture claims to protocol assumptions
- end with what should be read cautiously versus confidently

## Abstract job checklist

A strong abstract usually does five things:
- frames the field-level problem
- bounds scope without a long definition paragraph
- names 2-4 stable lenses (not a ten-item inventory)
- states what the survey contributes to reading the literature
- ends with a concrete takeaway about comparison, evaluation, or risk

## Keep out of the abstract

Avoid:
- `This survey provides an overview [generic opener]`
- `We organize the paper in the following sequence`
- long method-log details
- a trailing citation dump line
- count-based slot phrasing (`Three takeaways [slot phrase]`)

## Useful compression rule

If a sentence only tells the reader what section will come next, cut it or replace it with a content claim.
